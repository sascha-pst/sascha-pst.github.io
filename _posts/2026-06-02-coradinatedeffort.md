CORA's volunteer training starts soon. Forty-some hours on the architecture of crisis. How to sit with a person on the worst day of their life and not flinch, not rush to fix, not crowd the silence with your own discomfort.

The legal advocacy, the safety planning, the long unlearning of the gap between what you want to say and what is actually useful. And then there is the module on suicide prevention, which I am most nervous about, more than some of the foolish things I've done.

It is not something I'm unfamiliar with. But that's an oblique way of saying it, it's how I feel about it. The thing is, when you are near it and then asked to be steady for someone else, it is not the same line nor axis.

In one dimension, you survive. The other, you are supposed to be useful inside of, to stay in orbit, again and again.

When I was younger I believed, with absolute certainty, that suicide was the ultimate selfish act. I held that belief the same way you hold a handrail on a staircase. It depends how familiar it is.

I am not sure selfishness explains anything about the lowest place a person can dwell in. It is not anywhere anyone chooses to be. It is where they are, for the time being.

So I keep finding reasons to avoid thinking, reflecting. Suicide prevention is the most challenging. Parts of me would rather guard the wounds than risk holding them in front of a stranger looking for something to hold on to.

But the word keeps turning back on me. If it meant anything at eight, it means something now. To sit out the work because it might cost me something I never had, is the more selfish position.

So here's to trying. It's not ok. It is what it is. And I want to do anyway. Looking at it forwards, backwards, and around, trying to be present.
