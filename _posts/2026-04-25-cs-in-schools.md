Half of California's high schools don't offer a single computer science course. The state contains Silicon Valley.

The [2024 State of Computer Science Education report](https://advocacy.code.org/stateofcs) put California at 52% of high schools offering CS for 2023–24, up from 45% the year before and 42% in 2018–19. National average: 60%. [California sits in the bottom fifteen states](https://blog.csba.org/cs-report-24/). About 5% of California high schoolers actually enroll in CS in any given year. The modal student graduates without one.

The distribution is where this stops being a slow-rollout story and becomes something else. [Schools serving high proportions of Black, Indigenous, Latinx, or Pacific Islander students offer CS at 34%; majority white and Asian schools, 52%](https://csforca.org/accessreport/). Low-income schools are three times less likely than high-income schools to offer it. [Rural schools sit at 34% offering CS, versus 58% for urban and suburban schools](https://blog.csba.org/cs-report-24/). Two-thirds of California students sitting AP Computer Science are boys.

The legislative trajectory matches. California adopted Computer Science Content Standards in 2018 and a Strategic Implementation Plan in 2019; both are voluntary. Assemblymember Marc Berman has introduced a bill requiring every high school to offer CS in three consecutive sessions — AB 1054 in 2023, [AB 2097](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billTextClient.xhtml?bill_id=202320240AB2097) in 2024, [AB 887](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?bill_id=202520260AB887) in 2025. The first two died in Senate Appropriations. [The Department of Finance opposed AB 2097](https://edsource.org/2025/california-lawmakers-try-to-again-make-computer-science-mandatory/729555) on the grounds that implementation would cost an estimated $50–73 million annually. AB 887 was then amended in committee to remove the offering requirement entirely; the surviving version directs the California Department of Education to produce a "computer science implementation guide" by July 2026. Even that stripped-down version was [held under submission in Appropriations on August 29, 2025](https://calmatters.digitaldemocracy.org/bills/ca_202520260ab887).

The standard pitch for closing the gap is workforce: [45,000 open computing jobs in the state as of January 2023](https://www.cde.ca.gov/nr/ne/yr24/yr24rel07.asp), against roughly 9,000 CS graduates a year. Framed that way, the constituency narrows to people who care about Silicon Valley's hiring funnel. Framed as education equity, it joins a much older fight — one with legal infrastructure, organizing precedent, and a half-century of data on what happens when states let resources distribute themselves along race and class lines.

A kid who takes one CS course in high school and never writes another line of code has still learned what an algorithm is, what data does, where decisions get made. That is closer to civics than to vocational training. It is also harder to fundraise around.

Half of California's high schools don't offer a single computer science course. The state has the data. Three legislative sessions have produced one stripped-down implementation guide held in committee.
