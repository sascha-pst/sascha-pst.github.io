Half of California's high schools don't offer a single computer science course. The state contains Silicon Valley.

The numbers are older than you'd think. 65 percent of California high schools offered no computing classes when the Level Playing Field Institute looked in 2016. Latinx and African American students were 59 percent of the K-12 population and 11 percent of AP Computer Science A test-takers. Female students were 49 percent of enrollment and 24 percent of AP CS A. These figures are the *premise* of the 2018 California computer science standards: the gap they were written to close. Eight years later, no one has made a serious public claim that the gap has moved.

The standards document exists. It is roughly 150 pages. The California Department of Education adopted it in September 2018; you can download it from cde.ca.gov. It defines five core concepts and seven core practices, and maps to the national K–12 Computer Science Framework. Its thesis is that understanding computer science as a foundational discipline is what California's students need to compete in their own state's economy.

So why doesn't a 150-page standards document make it into the curriculum?

There is no graduation requirement. No state assessment measures whether students have encountered the material. Math is mandated, tested, and tied to school dashboards; CS is "available." More than a thousand California districts can adopt or ignore.

Assembly Bill 873, signed October 2023, was widely covered as California's K–12 media literacy mandate. The operative language requires the Instructional Quality Commission to *consider incorporating* media literacy content into curriculum frameworks when those frameworks are next revised. Frameworks revise on staggered seven-year cycles. AB 2876, the 2024 AI literacy follow-up, uses the same template. Both findings cite urgency. Both remedies authorize a commission to consider whether to recommend that future textbook adopters consider materials that include the topic.

This is not legislation. It is the appearance of legislation, drafted to avoid the constitutional reimbursement requirements that real mandates trigger.

The actual curriculum work happens elsewhere. The 2025 CDE AI guidance points at AI4K12, led by faculty at Carnegie Mellon and the University of Florida, and at CSTA. Their joint AI Learning Priorities project, NSF-funded, articulated five categories of K–12 AI learning. CSTA's findings will inform the 2026 update of its national CS standards, which California districts may or may not adopt. The substantive work, figuring out what a fifth grader should understand about machine learning, was done by volunteer experts on grant money. The state's role was to point at it.

That is how most US standards work, from the Common Core to NGSS. The problem is what California chose to do with the part the state is actually responsible for: funding implementation, training teachers, ensuring students encounter the work. AB 1251 (2023) created a commission to study how to recruit CS teachers. Teacher prep programs do not require CS preparation.

The distribution of who gets CS instruction is the distribution of who has slack to opt in. Affluent suburbs near tech employers run robust programs; rural and underfunded urban districts run none. 86 percent of students in small-town and rural California districts told Google and Gallup in 2017 they expected to have a job requiring CS knowledge. Their principals were 7 percent less likely than suburban and urban principals to call CS a priority. The students know what is coming. The system does not.

It is tempting to call this hypocrisy.

Hypocrisy implies a gap between stated values and behavior. The behavior here is the value.

California has a functioning political economy of educational standards: legislators get credit for passing bills, agencies for adopting frameworks, professional associations for the curriculum work; districts absorb implementation costs or do not. Each layer performs as designed. The students at the bottom of the stack are not a failure of the system. They are the negative space the system was built to leave.

The 150-page document is not the achievement. It is the alibi.

The fifth-grader in Bakersfield whose school cannot staff a CS teacher is, in the official record, being prepared for the future.

She is not. But the paperwork is in order.
