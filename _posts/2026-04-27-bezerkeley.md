In a normal life, a life I warmly recall and which I am reminded that other people still have: mornings are for work, evenings are for not-work, and weekends are for whatever you didn't do during the week.

In a working-professional graduate program, mornings are for work, evenings are for class or coursework. Weekends are for the coursework you didn't finish on weeknights, plus the work emails that piled up whilst doing the coursework. Breaks are for catching up on all the things that add depth and meaning to all of the above, as evidenced by the 10 tabs of archives courtesy of the UC library system, and this website, built in the break between spring and summer semester.

The promise of a graduate program designed for working professionals is that you don't have to choose. You can keep your career, you can get the credential, you can do both. And it is true, in the narrow sense that the credential is real and the career continues to exist.

What the Instagram ads don't quite picture is that the way you make this work is by paying for it in time you didn't know you had to spend. To be fair: it's better than the ads suggest.

The cost shows up in places you weren't watching:

The hour I used to spend on X, gone. Books-for-pleasure are aspirational objects that live on a shelf and accuse me silently. The two hours I used to spend cooking on Sundays. Compressed to a one-pot bowl of quinoa and hearty vegetables. Thrown over rice or greens. Whatever is leftover, really.

The texture of weekends. A weekend used to have an arc - Saturday morning was different from Sunday afternoon. Now both days are essentially the same: a block of time during which I am supposed to be making progress on three things at once, and take the dog to the park.

The capacity to be casually present with people. This is the cost I underestimated most. When you have a quiz due Sunday and a project deadline Monday, you can technically still go to a friend's birthday on Saturday. You will be there. You will not be there there. There's a part of your brain doing background calculations the entire time about how much time you've spent and what it costs.

I don't say any of this with self-pity, I'd do it again, I am doing it again. But the professional-development genre of "yes-and" content tends to skip over what the trade-off feels like.

The cognitive load of context-switching. It's almost like going to work in one language, and coming home and doing all of your homework in another. It's a tired that comes from holding a work problem in your head all day, putting it down, and trying to pick up sustainable construction financial modeling at 7 PM.

It's mental gymnastics. Work: 4D chess. Grad school: trying to master crow pose when you've barely got the muscles for a pushup. And that 2 hours to drive to and from yoga? Forget it, the mat is well-loved by the computer and the ash from the incense keeps the plants happy.

Here's what's true: the work is hard, the cost is real, and the trade is mostly worth it, and it might not be worth it five years from now.

What makes it worth it isn't the credential, it's that I am, for the first time in a while, learning things that change the way I think.

Reading papers on algorithmic fairness in healthcare and having an opinion about the methodology — that's a great privilege, and I'm honored to be able to work at the same time.

I now know what I don't know in a much more precise way than before.
