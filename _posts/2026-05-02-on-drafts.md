I wanted to apply to graduate school in 2024. I didn't, until I was ready — five applications later. This is a post about the first version of application materials, and the small choices made in between. The first version begins like this:

*My professional journey has revolved around extracting insights from data and using them to drive strategic decisions, even before I formally recognized this as "data science."*

I read this sentence now and feel a small, specific tenderness. I didn't even know what boosting meant when I wrote it.

I wrote a lot of sentences like this: 
*Energized by the challenge of transforming raw data into actionable intelligence. A unique blend of global business acumen, marketing expertise, and a data-driven approach. I am driven by a desire for continuous learning and professional growth.* 
They are not bad sentences. They aren't anything. They are the median of all such sentences, smoothed by anxiety into a finish so consistent it reflects nothing.

I assembled, with care, a profile. When you submit a profile, you ask the reader whether you belong. When you submit an argument, you ask them to engage with a position you've taken about the field you want to enter.

I never submitted that profile. Instead I applied to a thousand-plus jobs and subscribed to the belief that going to school in your thirties was a wasted opportunity cost.

The second version opens with a failure:

*Despite significant investment and clear public value, the program lacked a quantifiable revenue driver, making it impossible to build a data-driven case for its continuation.*

The first version would have called this "an opportunity to develop measurement frameworks." The second calls it what it was: a thing that did not work, and deep regret for not fighting for something worth the energy to measure.

This person desperately wrote the draft alone in a hotel room after being denied transcripts she'd earned a decade ago. She'd flown from a wedding in Bali to Shanghai to accept admittance to Johns Hopkins.

What changed between drafts was not what I said. It was what I was willing to admit. The first version hid the year I took off. The second put Shanghai and India and the caretaking on the page and called them evidence. The second told a story about a personalization strategy that produced a siloed user experience, and said: *while data can win arguments, it cannot replace human empathy.*

They are the same person. The first version wanted to be liked. The second wanted to be admitted. The version writing this wants neither.

The only reason to revise anything — a sentence, a draft, a self — is that the previous version wasn't the truth, the drafts haven't stopped.
