The Peace Arch is uglier than you remember every time, but the tulip fields start almost immediately after, and you forgive everything.
This is the Skagit Valley in spring: rows of red and yellow running flat to the horizon, so saturated they look edited. You pull over because you have to. Everyone pulls over. There's an unspoken agreement on I-5 in April that the fields justify the detour, the slower pace, the stranger with a phone out standing in a ditch. You take a photo that will not capture it. You take it anyway.

Bellingham comes next and it's a good stop. Waterfront, farmers market, the energy of a town that made a decision about what it wanted to be and followed through. You get coffee. You walk a little. The air is still cool and smells like Douglas fir and recent rain.

The drive south on I-5 asks nothing of you. It's comfortable in the way that long familiar roads are comfortable, the kind of driving where your hands know what to do and your mind goes somewhere quiet. Everett. Lynnwood. The Seattle skyline does its brief thing and disappears. The radio finds something good right when you need it to.
Vancouver stays with you a little. Mountains behind a city. Ramen on a Tuesday. Transit that runs on time. You hold it the way you hold any place you like, loosely, knowing you'll be back.

But somewhere around Tacoma the road tilts toward home and the math shifts. Your own bed. Your own coffee. The dog's specific and total joy at your return, which is unreasonable in the best way.

The flowers were worth it. The whole drive was worth it. You remember this, for once, before you've even finished unpacking.
