§ 01

Problem

Workforce development organizations administer programs that touch millions of Americans annually: job training, certification pathways, wraparound services, job placement support. Most operate with limited visibility into whether their programs actually drive economic mobility for the people they serve.

Administrators face three structural data problems:

  • Fragmented data sources. Outcome data lives across CRM systems, government reporting portals (WIOA, state labor systems), training partners, and employer follow-ups, with no unified view.
  • Lagging indicators. Outcomes data (employment, wage growth, retention) often arrives 6–12 months after a program intervention, making real-time program improvement nearly impossible.
  • Reporting burden over learning. Existing data infrastructure is built for compliance reporting (federal/state mandates), not for program improvement, leaving admins data-rich but insight-poor.

The result: programs run for years without clear evidence of what works, who they work for, and where they fail.

§ 02

User & Use Case

Primary User

Workforce development organization administrators. Specifically: program directors at workforce boards, community colleges, and nonprofit training providers; operations leads at workforce-focused social enterprises (Year Up, Per Scholas-style orgs); grant-funded program managers responsible for outcome reporting.

Secondary Users (Deferred)

Caseworkers, funders, and job seekers themselves. The product roadmap may extend to these users, but v1 focuses on admins to keep scope tight.

Core Jobs to Be Done

  • Understand. Which programs are working, for whom, and why?
  • Diagnose. When outcomes drop, where in the participant journey is the breakdown?
  • Act. What program changes are most likely to improve outcomes for which subpopulations?
  • Report. How do I produce funder, board, and regulatory reports with less manual work?
§ 03

Product Concept

Meridian is an AI-orchestrated outcomes intelligence platform that connects across an organization's existing data sources (CRM, training systems, government reporting feeds, employer follow-up data) and gives administrators three capabilities:

  • Unified outcomes view. A single interface showing program-level and cohort-level outcome metrics — employment rates, wage outcomes, retention, certification completion — stratified by participant demographics and program design choices.
  • Agentic diagnosis. AI agents that proactively surface anomalies ("placement rates dropped 12% for participants under 25 in Q3") and trace likely causes through the participant journey.
  • Recommendation workflows. AI-generated, evidence-grounded recommendations for program changes, drawing on the org's own historical data plus a curated knowledge base of workforce development research.

The "agentic" framing is intentional. Meridian doesn't just visualize data — it actively does the diagnostic and recommendation work that admins currently do manually, or don't do at all.

§ 05

Out of Scope (V1)

  • Direct services to job seekers. Search, matching, application support. Valuable but a different product.
  • Integrated case management. Meridian sits above existing case management tools, not replacing them.
  • Predictive forecasting at the individual participant level. Raises algorithmic fairness concerns that deserve dedicated research before shipping.
§ 06

Open Questions

This is a concept-stage document. The following require user research before v1 scoping.

  • Which data sources are most commonly fragmented across target organizations? Hypothesis: state WIOA reporting + internal CRM. Needs validation through admin interviews.
  • What's the realistic technical capacity of target orgs to integrate a new platform? Many workforce nonprofits run on minimal IT staff. Integration burden could be a deal-breaker.
  • What's the procurement model? Many target orgs are grant-funded with constrained software budgets. Pricing structure needs to fit funder cycles.
  • What's the build-vs-buy alternative landscape? Existing tools include Salesforce-based CRMs (Apricot, ETO) and state-level WIOA reporting systems. Meridian's wedge needs sharper definition.
§ 07

Responsible AI Considerations

Outcomes intelligence in workforce development is a high-stakes domain. Algorithmic recommendations that influence program design can systematically disadvantage subpopulations if not carefully constructed.

Meridian's AI components should be built with:

  • Disaggregated reporting by default. Outcome reporting by race, gender, age, and disability status — surfaced as default views, not opt-in filters.
  • Transparent reasoning. AI-generated recommendations show their reasoning chain. No black-box outputs.
  • Human-in-the-loop approval. Any recommendation that would be implemented requires admin review and approval.
  • Regular fairness audits. Recommendation patterns audited across subpopulations on a defined cadence.

This connects to ongoing parallel research on algorithmic fairness in healthcare AI screening. The same evaluation principles that apply to mammography AI bias apply here: disaggregated metrics, fairness-aware methods, and skepticism toward "neutral" optimization.
