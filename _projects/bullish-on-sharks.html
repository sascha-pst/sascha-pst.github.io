Counts are on the rise, but largely in part by reporting coverage and beach use, not per-capita risk. Death by shark attack is down for the count.

Key findings

Five things the data actually says.

Each claim is paired with the caveat it deserves. None of this is risk in the P(attack | hour in water) sense, GSAF has no exposure denominator.

i.

Fatality rate has fallen sharply across the 20th century.

Despite an uptick in raw attack counts (which rise with reporting and beach use), the share of attacks ending in death declines across the decades. This is consistent with faster medical response and beach patrols, not any change in shark behavior.

ii.

Swimmers die at 3× the rate of surfers — given an attack.

Surfers are bitten more often but survive most encounters (boards as flotation, proximity to shore, frequent mistaken-identity bites). Swimmers are more often in deeper water without flotation. Divers not considered in dataframe.

iii.

Geography concentrates counts, and water use.

The USA, Australia, and South Africa recorded highest number of incidents. All countries with coastal recreation, substantial reporting infrastructure, and longest GSAF coverage. High counts ≠ high per-swimmer risk.

iv.

White, tiger, and bull sharks are associated with fatal outcomes.

In the logistic regression, these three categories carry the largest positive coefficients on fatality. Species is also vulnerable to reporter bias; 45% missing, and witnesses aren't ichthyologists.

v.

Male victims outnumber female victims roughly 9:1.

Almost certainly exposure-driven (surfing, spearfishing, commercial fishing demographics) rather than a shark-side effect. Nothing in this analysis or other literature suggest a gendered preference.

Temporal patterns

Counts rose. Lethality fell.

Two lines tell competing stories. One is mostly reporting infrastructure catching up. The other survives that bias.

Chart 01 · Recorded attacks per year 1900–2023

Attacks rise, but so does everything else

Beach population, reporting coverage, and GSAF's collection practices grew over this window. Treat as a lower bound on recorded activity, not a risk curve.

Chart 02 · Fatality rate per attack, by decade P(fatal | attack)

The cleaner signal points the other way

Better reporting should raise the share of non-fatal attacks captured. A falling rate across decades is therefore hard to explain as pure reporting bias.

The conclusion isn't that sharks changed. It's that medical response, evacuation, and beach patrols did. — Finding i.

Model

Logistic regression, with humility.

Fatality ~ activity + species + country. Rare species (< 30 attacks) dropped to avoid perfect separation. Sensitivity check restricted to unprovoked attacks holds the same direction on the main coefficients.

Chart 06 · Coefficients on fatality (log-odds) pseudo-R² = 0.18

What predicts a fatal outcome, conditional on attack

Positive bars raise fatality odds; negative bars lower them. All relative to the reference category in each group.

The regression is associational, not causal. Activity, species, and country are entangled: surfers in California meet different sharks than spearfishers in Papua New Guinea. Methodology note
Read this before citing anything

Caveats worth restating.

  • No exposure denominator. All "risk" numbers are P(fatal | attack), not P(attack | hour in water). Without hours-in-water by activity and country, absolute risk is out of reach.
  • Reporting bias is pervasive. Early-20th-century decades, non-English-speaking countries, and non-fatal attacks are systematically under-recorded. Treat recent data as less biased than older data, not unbiased.
  • Species and time are ~45–51% missing. Species-specific claims are suggestive only. Time-of-day findings describe the reported subset, not the full incident set.
  • The model is associational. Activity, species, and country are correlated. Coefficients reflect partial associations within this joint distribution, not causal effects.
